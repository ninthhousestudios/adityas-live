--- tvashta | aditya

The Love of Creating Useful Forms

Tvashta was the one who trimmed the rays of Vivasvan so that humanity could
endure the Sun without being burned. He acts as the designer and architect of
the gods, creating various weapons and other objects that are useful to others.
In some instances, Tvashta created something with a personal intent: not to help
another or as a service, but rather to fulfill his own desires. These forms were
not nearly as successful and ended up causing many problems.


=== Reflections

How often do you make something for other people, or buy something for other
people?

Do you enjoy receiving gifts? What are your favorite kinds to receive?

How often do you make things? Do you do it with your hands, with a computer, or
just in your imagination?


--- jamadagni | rishi

Paternal Law

Jamadagni represents Paternal Law. One of the prime purposes of this law is to
assure that we are only responsible for our own actions and choices. We are born
into a certain lineage through no fault of our own and inherit many tendencies
from that lineage. We also may be burdened by past misdeeds carried out by our
fathers or forefathers. But to the extent that we are enmeshed in our hereditary
traits, we are not responsible for this itself. Yet our lineage also carries
latent gifts — abilities and potentials that may remain hidden until the right
circumstances draw them out. How we relate to what we have inherited, whether we
embrace it, rebel against it, or build upon it, shapes which of these tendencies
come to the surface.


=== Reflections

What is your relationship to your family? Were you rebellious as a child or
teenager, or did you stay within the family bounds?

When growing up, did you look up to your mother or father? Did you want to be
like them, or nothing like them? How are you turning out — just like them,
completely different, or perhaps an improved version?

Do you feel connected to your ancestors and lineage? Do you know your heritage
and the history of your family? Or do you dislike your history and what your
ancestors did?


--- shatajit | yaksha

The Body's Purpose

At Vivasvan we realize how fragile our individual bodies are and how much we
need other people; the focus is on ourselves. When we get to Tvashta and his
yaksha Shatajit, we are now tasked with actually using the body according to its
proper functioning, which is to help others and contribute to our community and
its health and wealth.

At Vivasvan we need help, and at Tvashta we are tasked with being the ones who
help. We help others by using our bodies in useful ways, such as making things
or providing services. When we do this because we want something in return for
what we have given, things can often go awry.


=== Reflections

How often do you do things for others? Do you regularly do any kind of volunteer
work?

Do you expect your children or your partner to fetch things for you, or are you
always the one fetching for them?

Are you the one who provides more for others through your body, or receives more
from others through the body? Or perhaps you have a good balance, where you are
able to give and receive in measures that are adequate to you?


--- brahmapeta | rakshasa

The Struggle of Self-Control

Brahmapeta was not a typical rakshasa. He lived surrounded by a variety of
terrible demons, but he himself had a refuge on the banks of the Manasa lake.
"Manasa" means "mind." Brahmapeta was not interested in following his baser
impulses like his other rakshasa cousins, but rather devoted himself to
self-control. He was a devotee of Kubera, the lord of wealth, and the son of
Praheti, the rakshasa of Aryama, who helps control our murderous rage.

=== Reflections

How disciplined are you? Can you control your actions or emotions, or are you
completely out of control?

In which areas of life do you have the most discipline? Where are you the most
focused, the most determined? Where are you both willing and able to sacrifice
for a greater goal?

In which areas of life do you have the least discipline? What temptations keep
pulling you away from the things you know you should do?

Have you ever brought yourself under control, moving from lack of discipline to
discipline? How did you do it? Where do you need to apply that same technique in
your life right now?


--- dhritarashtra | gandharva

He Who Is an Expert Judge of What Is Truly Valuable

The gandharva force is the energy that we put out into the world that causes
things to happen. How do we know what is worth doing? There are so many things
we may want to do or be required to do, and usually that is too much to actually
accomplish. In order to deal with this, we have to understand what is valuable.
Value is not just what something is worth in itself, but must also include a
recognition of the cost, because no matter what we may gain, there is always a
cost.

Dhritarashtra means "one whose empire is firm." This is a powerful king who
knows where to put his resources. To do this he requires a keen sense of what is
truly valuable, allowing him to understand the costs and tradeoffs for all of
his actions and decisions.


=== Reflections

What do you value the most? What do you value the least?

Do you see value primarily in terms of your emotional relationship with
something, your physical relationship with it, what it gives to you, or what
you give to it?

What do you think is the purpose of life? How do you decide what is valuable?


--- tilottama | apsara

The Uncontrollable Appreciation of the Innate Beauty of Everything

Tilottama appears to seize you with awe and wonder. You can see into the heart
of something and understand its essence, and therefore appreciate the value that
thing offers. Every thing in creation has an inherent value, and Tilottama
inspires in us the recognition of that value in whatever form it may happen to
have. Her name means "the best sesame seed." Sesame seeds are a highly
nutritious and powerful food, even though they are small. They are very valuable
regardless of their appearance.

=== Reflections

When was the last time you were struck dumb with awe? When you witnessed
something, or felt something, and it just consumed you with its beauty?

Do you have any gratitude practices? Do you regularly think about the things you
enjoy and cherish and appreciate in your life?

How often does a sense of awe at how your life is going and how creation
operates well up in you? If never, why do you think that is? Do you focus mostly
on the negative?

Think about when and how you experience joy. How can you bring that into your
life more fully and more consistently?


--- kambala | naga

The Urge to Find What Is Undyingly Valuable

Dissatisfaction could be a sign of several things, one of which is a sincere
recognition that what we have, are, or do is not as valuable as we would like it
to be. Often this causes us to run after things in the external world that we
think will provide us with value: a different, newer, better job, house,
partner. These may apparently satisfy for a while, but then they become less
valuable than before and we seek out something else.

This urge itself, to find something truly and undyingly valuable, comes from
Kambala naga. This urge is not itself a problem, because ultimately it will lead
us to find what is truly valuable, though we will make many mistakes along the
way.


=== Reflections

Are you satisfied with your life? Not that you think it is a good life and you
are grateful for it, but do you feel your life is truly valuable? If so, what
value does your life have?

What is really valuable, and how do you know? Have you thought something was
valuable but it turned out not to be?

How often do you examine what you have in your life — objects, thoughts,
emotions — and try to determine whether this thing is truly helpful to you, or
whether it is holding you back?

Are you satisfied with being satisfied, with simply being okay?
